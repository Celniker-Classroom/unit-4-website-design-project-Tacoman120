Why You Should Play

This page is just a summary of why I think playing Magic: The Gathering is worth it, and my favorite parts of the game.

Gambling

Who doesn't love gambling? I know I do. Magic: The Gathering is full of opprotunities to gamble, and a equal number to avoid it if you don't want to. The gameplay in itself is inherently gambing, as you are drawing cards from a randomly shuffled deck, meaning every game you have a different chance of winning or loosing. That isn't to say it is a luck game. Infact, many cards in the game allow you to pull the exact cards you want. However, if you want to rely on luck and "The Heart of The Cards", you totally can. There are two main opprotunities to gamble. The first one is in the sealed format, which is a format of Magic where you play with what are called draft booster or booster packs in the more recent sets. Each person opens a pack and takes one card from it before passing it another person, which continues until there are no cards left, and then another pack is oppened. By the end of this process, each player should have around 40 cards, which they then turn into a 40 card deck including lands, meaning that only about half the cards make it into the deck. This provides a truly unique playing experience and is very popular to do at stores. The other way to gamble is to open packs, the true essence of a trading card game. There are two main types of packs, the booster packs and the collector packs. Booster packs allow you to gain low value cards for adding to your decks. On the other hand, collecter packs allow you to draw cards of real value, with rarer cards going up to 1000$ in price. The most expensive Magic card ever sold was the one of one ring, from the Lord of The Rings set, with only one copy ever printed. This card was bought by Post Malone for about 2 million dollars, but the final amount was undisclosed.

Satisfaction

Every game is like a puzzle. You're constantly making decisions—when to attack, what spells to hold, how to predict your opponent. When a plan works (like setting up a combo or baiting out a counterspell), your brain gets that strong “I figured it out” satisfaction. Building a deck is like building a strategy that reflects how you think. Whether you like aggressive plays, long control games, or weird combos, the deck becomes an extension of your personality. Winning with something you built yourself feels much better than just “using something.” Magic gives frequent “mini wins”like drawing the exact card you need, pulling off a combo, or outplaying someone in a single turn. These small hits of success keep you engaged the whole time. Playing against real people adds stakes. Reading your opponent, bluffing, and reacting in real time creates a dynamic that feels more alive than solo games. Even casual matches can feel meaningful.
